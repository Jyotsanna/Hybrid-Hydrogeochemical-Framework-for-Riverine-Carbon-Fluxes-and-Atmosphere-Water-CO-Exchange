# Results and Discussion

## Overview

This document summarizes the major findings of the hydrogeochemical investigation of the Cauvery River Basin. The results integrate hydrochemical characterization, water quality assessment, geochemical modelling, statistical analysis, and carbon system evaluation to understand the processes controlling river water and groundwater chemistry.

---

# 1. Hydrochemical Characteristics

The hydrochemical analysis indicates considerable spatial and seasonal variation in water chemistry across the study area. The concentrations of major cations and anions varied according to geological setting, weathering intensity, hydrological conditions, and anthropogenic influences.

The dominant dissolved ions observed include:

- Calcium (Ca²⁺)
- Magnesium (Mg²⁺)
- Sodium (Na⁺)
- Potassium (K⁺)
- Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻)
- Chloride (Cl⁻)
- Sulphate (SO₄²⁻)

These ions collectively determine the hydrochemical evolution of river water and groundwater.

---

# 2. Water Quality Assessment

The suitability of water for drinking and irrigation was evaluated using standard hydrochemical indices.

The analysis revealed that:

- Most sampling locations were suitable for domestic use after considering major ion concentrations.
- Several locations exhibited elevated Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) and hardness, reducing their suitability for drinking.
- Irrigation suitability varied according to Sodium Percentage (Na%), Sodium Adsorption Ratio (SAR), Residual Sodium Carbonate (RSC), and Permeability Index (PI).
- Water quality deterioration was more evident in regions affected by agricultural and urban activities.

---

# 3. Hydrogeochemical Processes

Several natural processes control the chemistry of the river system.

The principal mechanisms include:

- Silicate weathering
- Carbonate weathering
- Evaporation
- Ion exchange
- Groundwater–surface water interaction

Weathering reactions contribute significantly to dissolved ion concentrations, while evaporation increases ionic strength during dry periods.

---

# 4. Geochemical Modelling

PHREEQC simulations indicate that mineral dissolution and precipitation play important roles in regulating groundwater chemistry.

Saturation index calculations demonstrate that:

- Carbonate minerals approach equilibrium under several hydrochemical conditions.
- Silicate weathering contributes substantially to dissolved solute concentrations.
- Mineral-water interactions strongly influence chemical evolution.

---

# 5. Carbonate System Analysis

Carbonate chemistry calculations were performed to evaluate dissolved inorganic carbon dynamics.

The results indicate:

- Carbonate equilibrium varies spatially across the basin.
- Atmospheric CO₂ exchange influences river chemistry.
- Dissolved inorganic carbon represents the dominant form of transported carbon.
- Carbon dynamics are closely linked with weathering processes.

---

# 6. Statistical Analysis

Multivariate statistical techniques were applied to identify relationships among hydrochemical variables.

## Pearson Correlation

Correlation analysis demonstrated strong associations between several major ions, suggesting common geochemical sources.

## Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

Principal Component Analysis reduced the dimensionality of the dataset and identified dominant factors controlling water chemistry.

The first few principal components explained a large proportion of the total variance, indicating that weathering processes, anthropogenic inputs, and hydrological conditions are the primary drivers of hydrochemical variability.

## Hierarchical Cluster Analysis

Cluster analysis grouped sampling locations according to similarities in hydrochemical characteristics, providing insight into spatial variability within the basin.

---

# 7. Spatial Variability

Geographical Information System (GIS) analysis revealed significant spatial heterogeneity in water chemistry.

The observed spatial patterns correspond closely with:

- Geological formations
- Land use characteristics
- Topography
- Drainage networks
- Seasonal hydrological conditions

Spatial mapping proved useful for identifying regions with relatively poor water quality and potential environmental concerns.

---

# 8. Major Scientific Findings

The study demonstrates that:

- Hydrogeochemical evolution is controlled by multiple interacting natural and anthropogenic processes.
- Weathering reactions represent the dominant source of dissolved ions.
- Seasonal hydrological variability strongly influences water chemistry.
- Carbonate system dynamics are closely linked with hydrogeochemical evolution.
- Statistical analysis effectively distinguishes the principal factors governing water quality.
- GIS-based analysis enhances understanding of spatial hydrochemical patterns.

---

# Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the results.

- Temporal variability was limited to the sampling period.
- Carbon flux estimates depend on available discharge observations.
- Local anthropogenic influences may introduce additional spatial variability.
- Future investigations should include long-term monitoring to better quantify seasonal and interannual changes.

---

# Future Directions

Future work may include:

- Machine learning approaches for water quality prediction.
- Continuous water quality monitoring using sensor networks.
- Integration with remote sensing observations.
- Climate change impact assessment.
- Coupled hydrological and biogeochemical modelling.
- Expanded carbon budget analysis at basin scale.

---

# Conclusion

This study provides an integrated hydrogeochemical framework for understanding water quality and riverine carbon dynamics in the Cauvery River Basin. By combining field observations, geochemical modelling, statistical analysis, and GIS techniques, the research demonstrates how natural weathering processes, hydrological variability, and human activities collectively regulate river chemistry and carbon transport.

The computational methods presented in this repository aim to improve the reproducibility of hydrogeochemical analyses and provide a foundation for future studies in hydrogeochemistry, carbon cycling, and Earth System Science.
